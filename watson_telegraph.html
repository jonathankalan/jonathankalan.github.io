IBM's Watson beat Jeopardy, now it wants to win over Africa

The Imperial ‘scramble for Africa’ at the end of the 19th Century is being replicated at the start of this one as investors throw money at emerging companies, but one old hand is using strong data to invigorate the continent

Winning Image: 'Digital Migration' by Lawrence 'Shabu' Mwangi Photo: Lawrence 'Shabu' Mwangi

By Monty Munford

7:00AM GMT 23 Mar 2014

Follow

Comments

Three years ago an IBM computer easily beat two human experts at the US quiz game Jeopardy in a challenge that lasted three days.

The computer known as Watson, named after IBM’s first CEO, has since moved on with the company ploughing more than a billion dollars into its evolution, a point underlined by current CEO Ginni Rometty at last month’s Mobile World Congress.

In her keynote speech in Barcelona, Rometty unveiled the latest of the computer’s capabilities with the IBM Watson Mobile Developer Challenge, an initiative to motivate global mobile developers to tap into its brain and data-crunching capabilities to create new apps for smartphones.

The future for IBM appears to be linked with the success of Watson, but it is one the company is comfortable with. Its potential appears limitless, not only for mobile developers, but also for the people of Africa.

Last year IBM announced its first research centre in Africa and its 12th globally, based in Nairobi at the Catholic University of Eastern Africa. There, ‘IBMers’ from across the planet and from Kenya will work to find solutions to Africa’s grand challenges across energy, water, transportation, agriculture, healthcare, financial inclusion, human mobility and public safety.

“In the last decade, Africa has been a tremendous growth story, yet the continent's challenges, stemming from population growth, water scarcity, disease, low agricultural yield and other factors are impediments to inclusive economic growth.

“With the ability to learn from emerging patterns and discover new correlations, Watson's cognitive capabilities hold enormous potential in Africa – helping it to achieve in the next two decades what today's developed markets have achieved over two centuries,” said Kamal Bhattacharya, Director, IBM Research, Africa.

Watson is also a fundamental part of Project Lucy, IBM’s $100 million project that was announced last month, just before before Rometty’s keynote at Barcelona. This ten-year initiative will be an ecosystem that brings together academic, industries, agencies and startups in a joint effort to address the aforesaid African Grand Challenges.

One early success with the Nairobi Research Centre has been The World Is Our Lab’s photo competition that attracted more than 1,200 entries from more than 25 countries. At an IBM Colloquium in Nairobi this week, the three winners were announced, although any of the stunning 45 short-listed entries could have won.

'Boda Boda' - by Frank Odwesso, Kenya

IBM’s decision to open its first African research lab in Nairobi and the consequent Watson and Project Lucy initiatives is good news for the city and its ongoing efforts to become the ‘Silicon Valley’ of Africa.

While such a phrase is hackneyed, and ‘Silicon Savannah’ has been bandied around as a moniker that doesn’t really describe the tech hub that is forming there, it has helped Nairobi position itself as such a cluster of technology companies.

The city’s highly impressive iHub, an innovation hub for Nairobi’s burgeoning tech community is advised by two of the continent’s tech superstars, Erik Hersman and Juliana Rotcih, and will work with IBM to nurture the city’s talent and emerging startup scene.

The adage that we all know about buying IBM is as banal as naming Nairobi a Silicon Savannah, but with the world’s biggest data-cruncher throwing its weight behind Africa, it won’t just be Jeopardy experts who are won over by Watson.

The next decade is likely to bring incredible change to Africa and if IBM’s mission brings half the data benefits that it promises, then Watson will have achieved something that its first CEO, Thomas J. Watson, will undoubtedly have been very proud.

Time will tell, the marketers will tell you that no one ever got fired by IBM, but no one should ever take Africa for granted.
